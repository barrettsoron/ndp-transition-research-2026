## Segment 1: Steven Carter and the Just Vote No campaign

**Higgins:** Anybody that wants to work with us, we're more than happy to talk to so that we can deal with this referendum and make sure that it doesn't succeed. Alberta Liberals, long absent from the legislature, but keen to have a voice, are partnering with a well-known political strategist to build a Just Vote No campaign ahead of the anticipated October 19th referendum that will feature no fewer than nine questions. And that political strategist, Steven Carter, president of Decide Campaigns, joins us now. Mr. Carter, good to have you on Alberta Prime Time.

**Carter:** Thanks very much, Michael. I'm thrilled to be here.

**Higgins:** People who follow politics in this province know you've worked in the past with everyone from Daniel Smith to Alison Redford, Jodie Gondek. Quite the spectrum of politicians. So what brings you to a partnership with the Alberta Liberals?

**Carter:** Well, I think that everybody that you just mentioned would work together to oppose these referendum questions. They are tragic questions that are putting our society at risk and I think that we need to stand together and stand united opposed to them. The Liberal Party was a fantastic vehicle that we could work with that's available to us. The New Democrats are already doing their project, which I wholeheartedly endorse and encourage. And Thomas Lucas's doing his, which I also wholeheartedly encourage and endorse. There's lots of space here to oppose these referendum questions.

**Higgins:** So maybe less about ideology, more about marriage of convenience?

**Carter:** More about a marriage of inspiration. We are both inspired to attack these referendum questions and try and push them back. I know that people talk about the separation question as though it's the only question. It's just one of the separation questions. We are arguing that all 10 of these potential questions are in fact separation questions, just to different degrees. So that's what we're trying to do — push back together against the referendum questions.

**Higgins:** How much of a vehicle is this for fundraising?

**Carter:** Tax receipts are available from political parties and they're not available from third party advertisers. That is a financial advantage that political parties bring to the table. They're also able to spend at a different rate than third party advertisers. Third party advertisers are maxed out at $67,000. A political party can go up as high as $5 million. We don't anticipate being anywhere near that, but nonetheless, it does give us the opportunity to give our donors more bang for their buck.

**Higgins:** Where fundraising is concerned, what is it going to take to have an effective voice? How big of a machine do you see needing to be built?

**Carter:** Everyone who wants to join, every single person who wants to be a part of this initiative should be a part of this initiative. You don't have to join the Alberta Liberal Party to be part of it. You can be part of what we're calling a constellation campaign — if you're trying to convince your book club that they should oppose the referendum questions, then you're part of our team. Our team is broad. It is wide. It has the capacity to accommodate multiple different points of view. We've got people who used to be part of the UCP cabinet that have joined us to oppose these referendum questions. That's the type of breadth that we want to bring — that maybe the NDP couldn't bring. But that's the joy of working with a relatively neutral party like the Alberta Liberals.

**Higgins:** This can't come cheap though. You'll need to pack a bit of a dollar punch.

**Carter:** As much as we can raise. But the way we're trying to do this is to engage volunteers at the grassroots level to do the campaigning within their own neighbourhoods. This isn't designed to be a multi-million dollar advertising campaign like the government's going to do. This is designed to be tens of thousands of dollars — maybe that would add up to a million dollars in total.

You mentioned Thomas Lucas's campaign by the NDP. This could end up being a very crowded space. So what does that say about the degree of divisive debate that lies ahead of a referendum on October 19th, separation question or not?

**Carter:** Albertans need to have a robust discussion about these referendum questions. Do we really want to see immigrants unable to put their children in school? How many of us are second or third generation people that have come to this province that wouldn't have the opportunity to have our children educated? This is ridiculous. We must educate everyone in our province. We can't have a second class of citizen or a second class of resident. The same would apply with the idea of paying for healthcare. These things will come and bite us later. We have to find a way to oppose them and robustly have a conversation about what our province is going to look like in the future. And that's what this entire thing is based on — developing a common set of communication tools that will be able to be used across the province.

**Higgins:** So you've teamed up with the Alberta Liberals. What needs to happen now for the Just Vote No campaign to truly take shape and hit the ground running?

**Carter:** We need as many people as possible to come to our May 2nd AGM to learn about how the campaign is going to unfold and how they can be participants in the campaign. And then immediately after May the 2nd, assuming the completion of our partnership, we need as many Albertans as possible to participate — whether they're participating with Thomas Lucas, the NDP, or with us.

**Higgins:** And how do you expect the other side of the coin to respond to any of these campaigns — yours included?

**Carter:** I would expect that they will do what they always do, which is try and change the rules to ensure that we are less effective. We see that today with the boundary commission's changes. This is not a team of people that want to follow the rules. They're a team of people that want to manipulate the rules to be successful. And that's what we are tremendously afraid of — that ultimately the UCP will do what they want to do anyway, regardless of how this referendum ends up.

**Higgins:** All right, we'll have to leave it there. Appreciate your time this evening, Mr. Carter. Thank you.

**Carter:** Thank you so very much for reaching out.

*That's political strategist Steven Carter.*

## Segment 2: Politics panel — federal NDP and Avi Lewis

*From campaigns queuing up to oppose provincial referendum questions, to what's at stake for the federal NDP in the wake of this week's byelections. Time now for tonight's politics panel.*

*Joining us this evening: Najib Jud, political adviser and branch strategist who's worked on election campaigns at every level of government here and abroad; and John Santos, political analyst.*

**Higgins:** Let's begin with Steven Carter partnering with Alberta Liberals on a campaign opposing the Smith government's referendum questions. Najib, what's to be made of the rationale for tag teaming with a party long absent from the legislature?

**Jud:** It wasn't accurate to describe the Alberta Liberals as neutral — more like irrelevant. As taxpayers, we should be really offended by this. $600,000 isn't enough money to reach every Albertan, which you don't have to reach every Albertan — you only have to reach people who vote. And second of all, do we need a fifth player in this mess? Another organization wasting money on this. We're going to have the NDP and the UCP wasting money on this. You've got Thomas Lukaszuk's campaign and the separatists themselves, and now a fifth player confusing and muddying the waters. They've already got out there talking about "just say no" when nothing's been decided in terms of the question format. And it's even more confusing as it moves on — you've got "For Alberta, For Canada," "Stay Free Alberta," "A Sovereign Alberta Within a United Canada," "Forever Canadian." All of this stuff just confuses people more and more, and either people are going to check out or vote the wrong way.

**Higgins:** John, how do you gauge the impact of a campaign like the one Steven Carter is bringing to the table?

**Santos:** His point about fundraising advantages — the ability to issue tax receipts, the increased amount of money parties can spend — those are real benefits. Notwithstanding Najib's excellent point about whether we really need more taxpayer rebates flowing from political contributions. Fundraising is going to be one of the big metrics. Attendance at whatever events they hold will be another. But ultimately, how we're going to measure this is what kind of turnout we see when the referendum happens and what kind of results. Are we going to see an overwhelming defeat of either the separation question or the other policy questions? That will be the test of how successful any of these campaigns are.

**Higgins:** Najib, could this help breathe some new life into the Alberta Liberals? What's to be made of their foray into this debate?

**Jud:** No, it's going to have zero effect for the Alberta Liberals. What it will do is have people associate being against separatism as being a Liberal thing — which, in a conservative stronghold in Alberta, is not a good association. It's all very poorly contrived. Like I said, we don't need another fifth player muddying the water. We don't need more confusion on things that aren't even decided yet. And the last thing we need — and this should anger every taxpayer — is another organization looking to waste hundreds of thousands of dollars on this initiative.

**Higgins:** Thomas Lucas was busy addressing a packed house in Calgary on Wednesday.

**Lucas** *(clip)*: Everything's at stake. People ask me, "What do we have to lose?" And we have to lose everything. So we're not only fighting a political battle over here — we're fighting actually an existential battle for the future of not only Alberta, but frankly for the future of the rest of Canada.

*Lucas, whose successful Forever Canadian petition remains in limbo while being dealt with by a legislative committee, says he's starting a new referendum campaign ready to mobilize volunteers starting in May.*

**Higgins:** John, we've been talking about multiple groups organizing. How crowded a space could this actually end up being?

**Santos:** As Najib said, it's already quite crowded. But the difference with what Thomas Lucas has been doing is that it's a step removed from the big-P political, partisan arena. That's the kind of thing that is really going to bring people together. You know, Najib also mentioned the point about Carter's project associating with the Liberals — whatever the provincial Liberals may or may not stand for these days, there's still that historical association with the federal Liberal party. And a lot of the separatist sentiment in Alberta is driven by — or at least correlated with — antipathy towards the federal Liberal party. So I think even if Thomas Lucas's group continues to campaign or other civil society organizations come forward, that's actually where we're going to see some of the more sober conversations. Less politically charged, less tainted by partisanship. That's where the higher quality debate is going to be happening, because it's a step removed from the political arena.

**Higgins:** For there to be a higher quality of debate, how much money is it going to take for any of these campaigns to be an effective vehicle? Is there the money out there in Alberta right now to support any of that?

**Jud:** It's going to take millions. Steven Carter is saying that $600,000 or $67,000 isn't enough — so how much are the other organizations spending? The UCP is definitely going to spend millions. The NDP is going to spend a considerable amount. All the players that need to be present are already present. Thomas Lucas's campaign, which has a lot of momentum, is all that we need to really represent the Albertans who don't want to separate. That's the campaign that should be supported. The NDP are doing their job in opposition. The UCP is doing their job. The separatists are doing their job. We have all the players we need in this phenomenal waste of money and time to begin with. And the last thing we need is just another organization out there. And what they won't be doing is asking taxpayers to pay for it.

**Higgins:** John, how do you see the separatist movement responding to the growing number of campaigns pushing a pro-Canada narrative?

**Santos:** They're going to use it to further support their narrative. I can already see the future social media posts from the separatists saying, "More Liberals lining up against Alberta. This is why we need to separate." And I think the message I would say to anyone who wants to get involved is — think about the Hippocratic oath in medicine. The first obligation is to do no harm. With how complex this environment is, the potential for misinformation and disinformation is real. Is adding another voice or another message going to help, or might it actually inadvertently do harm to the very cause that people want to support? That's really the big question that people have to ask themselves.

**Higgins:** Time for us to take a bit of a break, but when we come back, how federal New Democrats are faring with the Carney Liberals now in a majority. More from our politics panel in a moment.

## Segment 3: NDP byelection results and Avi Lewis

**Higgins:** Welcome back. In the wake of this week's three federal byelections, we've discussed the road forward for the Carney government now that it has a majority, and what lies ahead for Conservative leader Pierre Poilievre after losing MPs to floor crossings. But what about Avi Lewis and the NDP?

> Canadians did not elect a majority government, and if they end up with one largely through floor crossings, it feels wrong and I think Canadians are disturbed by this.

While no new NDP MPs were elected Monday, the party's vote share was up in both Ontario ridings. The New Democrats also had their parliamentary funding reinstated this week after losing official party status in the federal election nearly a year ago.

Najib, what's to be made of how the NDP fared in Monday's byelections, even in comparison to the Conservatives?

**Jud:** I think it's too early to make this a referendum on Avi Lewis. He just became leader. But I do think we saw some positive signs for the NDP in these byelections. They did double their vote in Rosedale. I think a good showing overall. The NDP has to fight its way out of the wilderness, which it was left in by the last leader — becoming a party of consultants and corporates rather than a worker party. And the more they describe Avi Lewis as a radical, I think the more attractive it is for people who are looking for that radical left party that the NDP is supposed to be. It's supposed to be our worker party. It's supposed to be the Labour Party of Canada, and it's long lost its way. I think we need strong opposition. I'm a big believer in that and the NDP is part of that opposition.

I don't agree with Mr. Lewis that the floor crossings are not what Albertans or Canadians voted for. At the end of the day, the Liberals are doing what they have to do to attract people to their party. That's not an accusation against the Liberals — it's against the Conservatives, who can't seem to hold on to their MPs because they have an unattractive leader who should have resigned and appointed a new leader who would have instilled confidence in the Conservative party.

**Higgins:** John, on that point of leadership — it was late last month that the New Democrats picked their new leader. Anything to read into the results where Avi Lewis is concerned?

**Santos:** Probably not. He was the front runner. I think it was always a tough road for Heather McPherson to climb to beat him. But I think if the party members were smart, they would have voted for McPherson. The irony is that Avi Lewis is perceived to be this far-left radical that may as well have been a Marxist university professor, whereas the NDP leader who was most successful federally was Jack Layton, who actually had a PhD but was seen as a man of the people.

And I think this is the difficulty that Avi Lewis has ahead of him. He is from the far-left wing of the party — and Najib says that folks on the left are looking for that, perhaps — but part of the success of the federal Liberals has been because Mark Carney has been very pragmatic. The fact that he's been able to pull floor crossers from both the Conservatives and the New Democrats shows a very pragmatic approach to politics. And I think Canadians want to see politicians work together. They want to see politicians looking for practical solutions and not spouting off whatever stereotypical ideological message that leaders like Pierre Poilievre like to spout off. So I think if Avi Lewis thinks that he's going to grow the NDP by being the Pierre Poilievre of the left, he's sadly mistaken, and the NDP will continue to be irrelevant on the federal scene.

**Higgins:** Najib, no byelection wins this week — but how big of a win is it for the NDP caucus to have its parliamentary funding reinstated, one year after losing official party status?

**Jud:** I'm kind of torn on that. They only have six seats. They did lose official party status — they need 12 seats for that. And I think that should be reflected in funding. But on the other hand, I think we need a strong opposition. We need more than one opposition party. We need a strong opposition party, and it's good for democracy. It's good for Canadians to have those options and choices. So if that helps them in terms of funding, I think that's a good thing.

And I will disagree with John. As politics keeps moving to the centre and everybody's catering to this mythical centrist voter — you have centre-right and centre-left parties in these duopolies that exist in different countries, including Canada — I do think you need an anchor on the left. And I think the NDP members chose rightly, and I think it's reflective of what they see missing in representation: those true leftist values, those true progressive values that parties like the Liberals and Democrats around the world have left behind, orphaning that base. People who truly want working-class representation. I think that's why they chose Avi Lewis. And as much as they're going to try to paint him as a Marxist and a communist, I think if he does his job right — if he communicates his ideas properly — he can dispel a lot of that narrative.

**Higgins:** All right, gentlemen, we're out of time. Have to leave it there. Great to see you both. Thank you for this.

**Santos/Jud:** Thank you.
