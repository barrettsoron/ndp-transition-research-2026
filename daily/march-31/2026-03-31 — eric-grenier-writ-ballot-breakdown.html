How the NDP leadership vote broke down, ballot by ballot

Source: The Writ  ·  Author: Éric Grenier  ·  Published: March 31, 2026

Avi Lewis was the top second choice of three of four of his opponents' supporters

(Apologies to readers for two newsletters in a single day, but this just dropped in my inbox!)

On Sunday, Avi Lewis won 56% of the vote on the first ballot of the NDP leadership race. Heather McPherson finished second with 29.5%, followed by Tanille Johnston at 7.3%, Rob Ashton at 5.9% and Tony McQuail with 1.3%.

The NDP leadership vote was a ranked ballot, meaning members could rank as many of the five candidates as they liked on their vote. But since Lewis won on the first ballot, it was not necessary to go to a second, third or fourth to determine a winner.

But that doesn't mean the results weren't recorded.

Today, I was provided with a ballot-by-ballot break down of the NDP leadership vote by one of the leadership campaigns. They show how each candidate's voters ranked the other candidates from first to fifth.

The data shows that Lewis had broad support from each of the camps and was the top second choice of the supporters of McPherson, Johnston and McQuail, with only more of Ashton's supporters ranking McPherson second. The results also show that more members ranked Lewis at least somewhere on their ballot than any other candidate, and that he had more first ballot support than any candidate had combined first and second choice support.

Let's get into the numbers.

Cross-camp support and second choices

Lewis was the top second choice of three of his four opponents, taking:

  • 35% of the second choice vote of McPherson's voters
  • 37% of McQuail's voters
  • 50% of Johnston's voters

While more Ashton supporters preferred McPherson, Lewis was still the second choice of 26% of these members.

This seems to suggest that the race was not as polarized between Lewis and McPherson as appeared to be the case — 36% of Lewis's voters ranked McPherson second while 35% of McPherson's supporters ranked Lewis second.

However, Lewis and McPherson voters were more likely than other voters not to rank any candidate second. Nearly 19% of Lewis's voters ranked him and only him on their ballot, while 23% of McPherson's voters only ranked her.

Voter blocs and ideological patterns

Nevertheless, we do see some voter blocs in these numbers:

  • Ashton did best among McPherson's voters at 20%, while only 10–11% of Lewis, Johnston or McQuail voters ranked Ashton second.
  • Johnston performed very well among Lewis and McQuail voters with over 30% of their second choices, and less well among McPherson and Ashton voters.

To be simplistic, the Lewis, Johnston and McQuail voters were on the left flank of the membership while the McPherson and Ashton camps were on the more moderate side. Nevertheless, there was still a lot of cross-camp support for every candidate.

What a runoff might have looked like

Because we don't exactly know how the ballots flowed after the second ballot, it is difficult to try to map out how the contest would have played out if it went to a final ballot with just two candidates on it. But it's clear that playing out the process to a final ballot would have only made Lewis's victory all the more emphatic.

Lewis had more 1st ranked ballots than any other candidate had first and second ranked ballots. Considering that McPherson was second on a lot of Lewis ballots, this shows just how much of a gap there was between him and the other candidates.

What's also revealed by these numbers is that McPherson did not have a realistic path to victory because of the weakness of the Ashton campaign — and not only on the first ballot. He was the least popular second choice among McQuail and Johnston supporters, so even had he finished ahead of them he would not have benefitted from their elimination.

It was only among Ashton's supporters that McPherson was the top second choice. If Lewis had not won on the first ballot, he would have benefitted most from the elimination of McQuail while McPherson would have gained some ground with the elimination of Ashton. But the elimination of Johnston would have easily put him over the top — Lewis had a 1,222-vote margin on McPherson among Johnston's second choices, while McPherson had only a 619-vote margin over Lewis among Ashton's second choices.

Ballot distribution across the field

Johnston's campaign was surprisingly strong. While she finished a distant third on the first ballot, she had nearly twice as many second ballot votes as did Ashton and still had more third ballot votes than him.

  • Lewis's votes were concentrated on the first ballot
  • McPherson's votes were concentrated on the first and second ballots
  • Johnston's votes were distributed across the second and third ballots

But Ashton had more fourth ballot choices than any of the candidates. He was more likely to be ranked fourth than he was second, while Johnston was more likely to be ranked second than she was third.

The bottom line

My back-of-the-napkin calculation is that if the other candidates had been eliminated to get to a final, two-person ballot, Lewis probably would have won by a margin of roughly two-to-one, so not much different than his actual margin.

That doesn't mean there isn't some dissent within his opponents' ranks. About 46% of McPherson's and Ashton's voters either ranked Lewis fifth or did not rank him at all. Some bridges will have to be rebuilt, but Lewis's support among the other camps, even those of his chief rivals, suggests the foundation might be stronger than even a first-ballot victory appears on the surface.
